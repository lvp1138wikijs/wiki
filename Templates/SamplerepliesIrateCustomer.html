5 Tips on How to Handle Angry Customers Without Losing Your Cool

  1. Resolve the problem. That is and should be your first and foremost priority.
  2. Be patient and never argue back. Try to get as much information from them as possible.
  3. Kill Them with Kindness.  Listen to their story without interrupting and then find a way to help.
  4. You have to make them feel it is personal. But you do not take it personally! 
  1. Know How to Apologize. Sometimes accepting our mistakes can do wonders.

> Speed/Computational power for shared hosting

Question : That still doesnâ€™t answer my question. Will it be faster for the end user to get my webpage on their computer, and by how much (eg. 200% faster, 400% faster, stc.)?

Reply : 

 

Hello Dave,

My name is Peter and I am the lead network engineer at ServerPoint.com.

The answer to you question is no. The reason for that is that ServerPoint.com does not throttle the transfer rate of your web site. It will send the website out to the client at the highest transfer rate that the recipient, and the path between us and the recipient, can handle.

Speed is actually not a term used on the Internet, because speed is just simply the speed of electrons going through a copper cable or photons going through fiber, and that is is always a constant speed.

The proper term to be used is actually capacity. How many bits per second can fit through the pipe. How many CPU cores are available, etc.

The difference between plans is resource capacity. Capacity of how much CPU is assigned to your account, how much RAM is assigned to each PHP process, and so on.

If your website hits those established capacities, then you will notice the transfer rate slowed down, because, for example, a PHP hit on your web site may need an entire CPU core for three second to process a purchase, but your hosting plan may only be assigned half a CPU core. That is just an example.

If a PHP hit on your ecommerce app requires half a CPU core, but you get ten hits at a time, and only one CPU core can be used by your plan, then you will hit a CPU limit, and thus, the PHP script won't be able to process the hit quick enough.

The other major difference is that the servers we used for the "cloud" hosting plan are actually more powerful machines. CPUs with higher clock speeds and more computational power, disk I/O that can handle more megabytes per second, and so on.

So, as long as your website does not hit such resource limits, you are good either in a Premium or Cloud plan. If it hits any of those limits, then you will notice the server won't process your applications as quickly as possible.

What are the limits you may ask. That is internal knowledge and proprietary, and those limits are mostly there to prevent abuse. For example, we don't want one single client's application taking over all of the CPU cores in a server, as that would essentially shut down the websites for every other client on the same server.

I hope that explanation helps.

Peter :)
